# Decision Science in Crisis Management

This companion directory supports the article **"Decision Science in Crisis Management."**

The workflows operationalize crisis management as a reproducible decision-science problem involving urgency, uncertainty, sensemaking, risk triage, escalation thresholds, resource pressure, public trust, cascading impact, response capacity, continuity planning, adaptive revision, and decision-record scaffolding.

## Core Claim

Crisis management is structured judgment under pressure: deciding what matters most, what is known, what remains uncertain, who has authority, which actions must happen now, which harms must be prevented, and how decisions should adapt as the situation changes.

## Modeling Principles

1. Treat crisis management as a decision system, not only command or response.
2. Separate confirmed facts, working assumptions, disputed information, and unknowns.
3. Use escalation thresholds for risk, uncertainty, public trust, resource pressure, and cascading impact.
4. Prioritize life safety, critical services, vulnerable populations, reversibility, and recovery capacity.
5. Treat public communication and trust as operational variables.
6. Map cascading risk across infrastructure, organizations, communities, suppliers, and services.
7. Use adaptive review cycles rather than locking into the first interpretation.
8. Preserve decision records for after-action learning.
9. Connect after-action findings to budgets, authority, training, exercises, and governance changes.
10. Use decision-support tools with humility, uncertainty disclosure, and human accountability.
