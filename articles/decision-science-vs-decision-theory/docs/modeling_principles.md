# Modeling Principles

## Formal rigor matters

Decision theory gives decision science a disciplined language for actions, states, probabilities, preferences, outcomes, utilities, losses, and decision rules.

## Formal assumptions must be tested

A formally correct model can still fail if probabilities are invented, preferences are hidden, values are contested, or implementation is impossible.

## Normative, descriptive, and prescriptive work are different

Normative models define rational coherence. Descriptive research studies how people and institutions actually decide. Prescriptive decision science designs better processes under real constraints.

## Robustness matters under deep uncertainty

When probabilities, models, values, or outcomes are contested, robust strategies may be more defensible than single-forecast optimization.

## Decision records preserve accountability

A decision record should document formal assumptions, evidence, alternatives, criteria, decision rules, rationale, dissent, and review triggers.
