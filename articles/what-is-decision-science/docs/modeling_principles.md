# Modeling Principles for Decision Science

## Define the decision before modeling options

A model should serve a defined choice. Without a clear decision, analysis can become technically impressive but strategically irrelevant.

## Distinguish outcome quality from decision-process quality

Good outcomes can result from poor decisions, and poor outcomes can result from sound decisions under uncertainty. Decision records should preserve the reasoning context.

## Make alternatives explicit

Decision quality depends on the quality of the option set. False binaries, missing alternatives, and premature convergence weaken the process before modeling begins.

## Represent uncertainty honestly

Probabilities, ranges, scenarios, and unknowns should reflect evidence quality. Invented precision is more dangerous than acknowledged uncertainty.

## Surface values and trade-offs

Weights, criteria, thresholds, and constraints embed value judgments. Decision science should make those judgments visible rather than hiding them inside technical models.

## Use sensitivity analysis to test assumption dependence

A preferred option is less defensible if it depends on fragile assumptions. Sensitivity analysis identifies where conclusions change.

## Prefer robustness over fragile optimization under deep uncertainty

When the future is structurally uncertain, the best strategy may be one that performs acceptably across many plausible futures rather than optimally under one forecast.

## Document decision records for accountability and learning

Decision records preserve assumptions, evidence, alternatives, criteria, rationale, uncertainties, and review conditions so that later learning is possible.

## Treat computational models as supports for judgment

Models can structure reasoning, but decision-makers remain responsible for values, interpretation, legitimacy, and consequences.
